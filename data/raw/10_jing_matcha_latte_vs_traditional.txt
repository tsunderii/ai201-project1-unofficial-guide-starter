JING Tea — Matcha Latte vs Traditional Matcha

Core difference
Traditional matcha consists of powder whisked with hot water only, while a matcha latte combines matcha powder with steamed or frothed milk and often includes sweetness. This single addition of milk fundamentally transforms the drink's flavor, texture, and cultural context.

Flavor profiles
Matcha naturally contains umami richness, vegetal notes reminiscent of steamed greens, and a balance of sweetness with subtle bitterness. The grade — ceremonial versus culinary — significantly impacts flavor intensity. Traditional matcha presents these characteristics intensely and layered, with no dilution masking the tea's nuances. In a matcha latte, milk proteins and fats soften the bitterness and create creaminess. Even unsweetened milk introduces natural sweetness, and many people add honey or syrups. Different milk types contribute distinct flavors — oat milk adds biscuit notes, almond milk brings nuttiness, and dairy offers classic creaminess.

Caffeine content
Both drinks start with identical matcha powder, so they share the same base caffeine profile supported by L-theanine. A standard serving uses approximately 1–2 grams of matcha powder, delivering roughly 60–70mg of caffeine. This is comparable to black tea but less than typical coffee. In lattes, the caffeine spreads across a larger volume, and milk's proteins and fats may slow digestion slightly, potentially making the energy feel more gradual.

Texture
Traditional matcha should be thin, silky, and light, with a fine micro-foam on the surface, feeling smooth like a delicate broth. Matcha lattes are noticeably thicker and creamier due to steamed or frothed milk, creating a cappuccino-like richness. Both should remain free from gritty clumps through proper sifting and whisking.

Preparation methods
Traditional preparation involves warming a tea bowl, sifting matcha to remove clumps, adding approximately 60–80ml of water heated to 70–80°C, then whisking briskly with a bamboo whisk in a "W" or "M" motion until smooth with fine foam. For a latte, create a matcha shot by sifting powder into a cup, adding 30–50ml hot water, and whisking until lump-free; heat and froth milk separately, then pour the frothed milk over the matcha shot, finishing with a spoon of foam on top.

Philosophy and context
Traditional matcha preparation is slow, deliberate, and ritualistic. Matcha lattes offer adaptability, working for leisurely savoring or quick morning preparation. Both require water around 70–80°C to avoid scorching the powder and accentuating bitterness.
